Abstract

Identifying farmers who prefer organic or chemical farming is essential for developing targeted interventions, supporting sustainable agriculture, and meeting the diverse needs of farming communities. One of the primary reasons for identifying these preferences is to enable better policy formulation and support programs. Governments and agricultural organizations need accurate information about farmers' practices to design effective subsidies, training programs, and extension services. Organic farmers, for instance, may benefit from programs that promote composting, bio-pesticides, and organic certification processes. On the other hand, farmers using chemical inputs may require support in safe pesticide use, soil health management, and integrated pest control techniques. Without knowing which farmers prefer which methods, support programs may be poorly targeted and less effective. Furthermore, identifying these preferences is vital for market development. Tracking the number and location of organic versus chemical farmers allows for better monitoring of environmental impacts, such as soil degradation, water pollution, and carbon emissions. Government can use this information to promote practices that enhance sustainability and reduce ecological harm
